Ways to Become Independent

This page will explore all of the Personal Circumstances questions on the FAFSA. If you are able to select at least one of these, you will be considered independent. Even if you are not independent by age.

Age

This question is not shown on the FAFSA any longer; but it is still calculated based upon the entered date of birth of the student on the demographics section of the FAFSA form.

If you are 24 years or older by January 1 of the award year, you are considered independent for FAFSA purposes.

You can check the award year in question by entering your Date of Birth on our home page to see if you would be independent. And you can also see which award year you would begin to be classified as independent solely based on your date of birth (i.e. Assuming you cannot answer any of these questions as Yes).

Married

This question is also not asked as a personal circumstance question any longer; but is still checked when the student enters their marital status in the demographics section of the FAFSA form.

Being married qualifies you as an independent student, regardless of your age. So for example, let's say if you are 18 years of age and you and your partner decide to get legally married. If you got married BEFORE completing your FAFSA, you are considered independent for FAFSA purposes.

Some people even believe that if you get married solely for FAFSA purposes, that for some reason, you would not be considered independent. This is wrong. If you are married as of the day you file the FAFSA, you are independent. Period.

Something to keep in mind: While you would be considered independent on the FAFSA. This would really only benefit you if both you and your spouse are low income earners. Especially if you have no children. The FAFSA will weigh income from independent students heavier than dependent students. Even more so if that independent student has no children.

Graduate or Professional Student

This question is also not asked as a personal circumstance question any longer; but it is still checked when the student marks their grade level in college as:
Master’s, doctorate, or graduate certificate program (MA, MBA, MD, JD, PhD, EdD, etc.)

Most likely you are not here for this question. As if you are independent by this manner, you most likely already have a Bachelor's Degree. Federal Grants, and all state grants we are aware of, are only applicable toward a student's first Bachelor's Degree.

For example: IF you already have your first Bachelor's Degree and you are beginning a Master's Degree program, you will only receive Federal Direct Unsubsidized Loans from the FAFSA. You will not receive any Federal Pell Grants. Even if your income is $0.

Additionally, let's say you have graduated with your first Bachelor's Degree, and you are now returning for a second Bachelor's Degree. Let's say you are now independent by age. You will still be ineligible for grants because you already have a prior Bachelor's Degree.

Currently Serving on Active Duty (Other than Training)

This personal circumstance question asks if the student is:
The student is currently serving on active duty in the U.S. armed forces for purposes other than training.

This question refers to student's serving on active duty in the United States Military only. Not a foreign country's military. You check-off this box only if you have been given active duty orders to serve. Being a reservist, or on active duty training, does not equate to Active Duty for this question.

Question: How do I know if my "Active Duty" is considered "Active Duty?"
A: Good question! You should check the box for this question if you meet any of the following types of Active Duty, as explained in 38 USC (101), which defines "Active Duty" status.

"(21) The term "active duty" means-

(A) full-time duty in the Armed Forces, other than active duty for training;

(B) full-time duty (other than for training purposes) as a commissioned officer of the Regular or Reserve Corps 1 of the Public Health Service (i) on or after July 29, 1945, or (ii) before that date under circumstances affording entitlement to "full military benefits" or (iii) at any time, for the purposes of chapter 13 of this title;

(C) full-time duty as a commissioned officer in the commissioned officer corps of the National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration or its predecessor organization the Coast and Geodetic Survey (i) on or after July 29, 1945, or (ii) before that date (I) while on transfer to one of the Armed Forces, or (II) while, in time of war or national emergency declared by the President, assigned to duty on a project for one of the Armed Forces in an area determined by the Secretary of Defense to be of immediate military hazard, or (III) in the Philippine Islands on December 7, 1941, and continuously in such islands thereafter, or (iii) at any time, for the purposes of chapter 13 of this title;

(D) service as a cadet at the United States Military, Air Force, or Coast Guard Academy, or as a midshipman at the United States Naval Academy; and

(E) authorized travel to or from such duty or service."

What Happens if I go from serving in Active Duty, to not serving in Active Duty?
A: Then you should be issued a Form DD-214 showing your discharge from Active Duty. This form is what will make you a Veteran for Financial Aid Purposes. You should review the section below for more information regarding veteran status.

Veteran

This personal circumstance question asks the student to check the box if:
The student is a veteran of the U.S. armed forces

Orphan or Ward of the Court

If you were an orphan, ward of the court, or in foster care after age 13, you are considered independent.

Emancipated Minor or Legal Guardianship

If you are or were an emancipated minor or under legal guardianship, this can qualify you as independent.

Homeless or At Risk of Homelessness

Being homeless or at risk can also qualify you as independent.

For more details, check the official FAFSA guidelines or contact your financial aid office.
